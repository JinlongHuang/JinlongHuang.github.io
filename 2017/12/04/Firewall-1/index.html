A Short Review of Firewall Problem

Posted on 2017-12-04 | Visitors

Firewall

The interior of a black hole has been a hot subject in recent year. There are many attempts to describe theexperience of an infalling observer. The first analysis is conducted by Susskind and Thorlacius[1]. They found outthat if we assume the evaporation of black hole is unitary, arbitrary quantum states that are thrown into the blackhole can both stay inside and reappear in the Hawking radiation, which violates the no cloning theorem. They alsopoint out that(later refined by Hayden and Preskill in [2]) no single observer can see both copies, which they calledthis idea black hole complementarity[1, 3–5]. But its consistency and precise definition was not clear.

Leonard Susskind

One argument against the consistency of complementarity is due to Almheiri, Marolf, Polchinski, and Sully [6], which is often referred as AMPS. In the early discussion of the information problem of black hole, some researchers concerned that changing the state of the evaporating modes of Hawking radiation would be dangerous to the infalling observer[7–10]. The term firewall called by AMPS is firstly suggested by Braunstein as “energetic curtain”[11]. Mathur [8] gives the main quantitative result of AMPS argument, based on the strong subadditivity of von Neumann entropy. AMPS illustrated that by Page’s theorem, once the black hole is sufficiently “old”, the black hole BH and its early radiation R should be maximally entangled. On the other hand, Rindler decomposition requires mode B near the event horizon outside the black hole should be entangled with a mode inside the black hole H. Thus a contradiction for quantum entanglement monogamy(EM) occurs. To resolve this problem, a firewall between mode B and H to eliminate their entanglement is proposed. A review of quantum physics of black holes and firewall paradox can be found at [12].

EM inequality is a fundamental restriction describing how entanglement distributes among multiple parties. In three qubits case, Coffman et al. [13] showed that there is a trade-off between A’s entanglement with B and its entanglement with C. Quantitatively speaking, the tangle between A and B, plus the tangle between A and C, cannot be greater than the tangle between A and the pair BC, where tangle is defined as the square of concurrence. As a special case, when A and B is maximally entangled, either of them cannot be entangled with C, even with a small amount. After this work, Osborne and Verstraete [14] proved a general monogamy inequality for the distribution of bipartite quantum entanglement among n qubits. In addition, Koashi and Winter [15] investigated other forms of monogamy of entanglement, which also includes classical correlation.

In 2013, a new proposal to address this contradiction for EM is suggested. Harlow and Hayden (HH) [16] proved that, under some assumptions, the computational complexity for Alice to “distill” the purification R_B requires an exponential amount of time, assuming the unitary only applies to early Hawking radiation R. For a black hole with the mass of our sun, its lifetime is 10^67 years, while it takes Alice more than 2^ (10^69) years [17] to extract R_B. At that time the black hole has already evaporated and she can no longer witness a violation of EM, i.e. observing both maximal entanglement between R_B and B and entanglement between mode B in later Hawking radiation and mode H inside the black hole. Therefore firewalls are no longer needed.
